01 Approach

The hard part of high-risk AI isn't building the model. It's proving it won't fail.

That proof is the work — and it needs someone who can speak to both the engineering and the regulation. Most people do one side. I work across both. Three things I do:

01
Build & evaluate

Design, train, and stress-test ML systems — robustness under distribution shift, failure-mode analysis, evaluation that holds up to scrutiny. Applied AI research background, 24 peer-reviewed papers.

02
Map to frameworks

Translate systems into the language of ISO 42001, NIST AI RMF, the EU AI Act, and ISO 27001 — so the same system satisfies engineering review and regulatory obligation at once.

03
Produce audit-ready evidence

Risk files, control mappings, model documentation, and assurance artefacts that a regulator, an auditor, and an engineer can all read. ISO 42001 Lead Implementer; Lead Auditor in progress.

One belief underneath all three: governance is not a checklist — it is a control system. Organisations tend to stack frameworks, each with its own audit and owner, and gaps open at the seams — exactly where high-risk AI fails. The frameworks are really different views of one problem: what could go wrong, who is accountable, and how you prove it didn't. Map the controls across them and most of the work is shared; the small remainder is where the real risk hides.

Each framework asks the same questions in different language. Map them, and you get one control system instead of multiple audits.

ART /02

SR 11-7 vs SR 26-2 — model risk management modernized

As of June 2026

On 17 April 2026 the Federal Reserve, OCC, and FDIC jointly issued SR 26-2, the first revision to US model risk management guidance in 15 years. It supersedes SR 11-7 (April 2011). The headline below isn't what's the same — it's what changed, and what the Fed deliberately left out.

Topic SR 11-7 (2011) SR 26-2 (2026)
What carries over 4 rows
Three-line-of-defence structure Model owners and developers (1st), independent validators (2nd), internal audit (3rd). Same intellectual architecture preserved. Independent review remains the core control.
Effective challenge Models reviewed by experts with independence, expertise, and organisational standing to question assumptions. Retained as a core principle — but the new guidance emphasises quality of challenge over rigid structural separation from developers.
Documentation and inventory expectations Documented model purpose, design, assumptions, limitations, and ongoing monitoring required. Same expectations carry; inventory expanded to support enterprise-level visibility into concentrations, dependencies, and aggregate risk.
Applicability threshold Most relevant to large banking organisations, with proportional application. Explicitly targeted at banking organisations with over $30B in total assets — proportionality formalised.SR 26-2, Applicability
What changed 5 rows
Length and prescriptive tone ~20 pages of prescriptive expectations. Treated by examiners as a de facto standard. Roughly half the length. Explicitly states the guidance does not set forth enforceable standards; non-compliance alone will not result in supervisory criticism.
Validation cadence Annual revalidation the de facto expectation across the industry. Annual revalidation no longer a default. Validation cadence is risk-based, tied to model materiality.
Model classification Binary "model vs non-model" determination, with rule-based tools often falling on the non-model side. Binary classification dropped. Materiality replaces the binary; institutions must assess model risk on a spectrum tied to purpose and exposure.
Inventory purpose A recordkeeping requirement, often satisfied as a documented list. Reframed as an enterprise risk artefact — must support visibility into concentrations, dependencies, and aggregate exposure.
Supervisory posture Examiners used SR 11-7 as the benchmark for sound practice; gaps drew supervisory criticism. SR 26-2 explicitly non-binding, but remains the benchmark for assessing sound practice. Tailoring is now expected, not just allowed.
What's now out of scope 3 rows
Generative AI & agentic AI models Predates the modern generative AI conversation; no explicit treatment. Explicitly excluded. Footnote 3 states they are "novel and rapidly evolving" and not within scope. Banks must apply existing risk management practices to govern them.SR 26-2, footnote 3
Traditional ML and statistical models In scope — gradient-boosted models, classifiers, neural networks for credit and fraud all governed. Fully in scope. Non-generative, non-agentic AI models explicitly remain under the guidance's principles.
The GenAI governance gap Not applicable. The carve-out doesn't shrink the governance problem — it pushes it onto enterprise risk frameworks that don't yet exist at most banks. A future RFI is signalled.

Primary sources: SR 26-2 letter (Federal Reserve) · SR 26-2 Revised Guidance (PDF attachment). SR 11-7 (2011) is superseded by SR 26-2; the new letter references and replaces it directly. The EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689) is a binding, risk-tiered law with enumerated obligations and penalties. The UAE AI Charter (July 2024) is a non-binding, principles-based instrument anchored to the UAE National AI Strategy 2031 — cross-sectoral, all 12 principles. Strategic Assessment 3 rows
Key strength Legal certainty: precise obligations, phased timelines, and clear penalty structure. Annex III provides a predictable classification system enabling systematic, auditable compliance programmes. Agility: principles-based approach allows rapid adoption without legislative lag. UAE's ability to embed governance directly into government services and public-private initiatives is a structural advantage.
Key gap High compliance overhead for SMEs and academic research. GPAI supply-chain obligations add complexity. Regulatory sandboxes remain underdeveloped relative to the Act's overall weight. The Digital Omnibus (May 2026) introduces tailored SME accommodations and reduces regulatory duplication for AI in industrial products. Non-binding nature creates uneven adoption across entities. No sector-wide AI audit baseline. Three UAE-unique principles (P8, P9, P10) currently lack operational implementation guidance.
Dual-jurisdiction convergence Seven of the UAE Charter's 12 principles map directly to EU AI Act provisions (P2 safety, P3 bias, P4 privacy, P5 transparency, P6 human oversight, P7 accountability, P12 legal compliance). Three UAE-unique principles — P8 Technological Excellence, P9 Human Commitment, P10 Peaceful Coexistence — have no regulatory counterpart in the EU Act. A dual-jurisdiction governance programme can share the majority of artefacts: risk registers, model cards, bias testing protocols, and data governance policies, with divergence concentrated in conformity assessment procedures and documentation formats.

REF /01

STPA applied to clinical AI

arXiv · Oct 2024

From Silos to Systems: Process-Oriented Hazard Analysis for AI Systems. Shalaleh Rismani, Roel Dobbe, AJung Moon. arXiv 2410.22526.

What they did. Adapted Leveson's System-Theoretic Process Analysis (STPA) for AI systems and walked it through three case studies — a linear-regression early-warning tool for late-onset sepsis in preterm infants, a reinforcement-learning insulin delivery system, and a transformer-based text-to-image model. They formalised the adaptation as PHASE (Process-oriented Hazard Analysis for AI Systems), with modifications for model opacity, capability uncertainty, and output complexity.

Why this matters. The clearest published demonstration that systems-theoretic analysis transfers cleanly to AI — and that the seams between model, deployment context, and human operator are where the failures actually live. The neonatal sepsis case in particular is a useful template for anyone designing a risk file for clinical decision support under EU AI Act high-risk obligations.

REF /02

HAZOP and STPA combined for a deployed autonomous shuttle

Automotive Innovation · Mar 2025

Cascaded Safety Analysis and Test Scenario Generation Techniques for Autonomous Driving: A Case Study with WATonoBus. Chen Sun, Ruihe Zhang, Ahmad Reza Alghooneh, Minghao Ning, Pouya Panahandeh, Steven Tuer, Amir Khajepour. University of Waterloo. Automotive Innovation 10.1007/s42154-024-00313-z.

What they did. Applied an integrated safety analysis to WATonoBus — Waterloo's all-weather autonomous shuttle that's been running daily weekday service on the campus Ring Road for nearly two years. HAZOP handles the operational flow (what could deviate in normal service); STPA traces those deviations through the control structure (how the deviations propagate); both are anchored to the system's operational design domain. The cascaded approach then generates test scenarios from the analysis, combining tree-search over discrete parameters with sampling over continuous ones.

Why this matters. HAZOP and STPA are usually presented as alternatives — one inherited from the process industries, the other from systems-theoretic safety. This paper shows them working as complements rather than competitors: HAZOP catches the operational hazards STPA's control-loop view tends to miss, and STPA traces the system-level consequences HAZOP can't reach. Anchoring both to the operational design domain is what makes the pairing operational, not just methodological.

Process FMEA applied to the ML development pipeline

The ML FMEA: A Safe Machine Learning Approach. Paul Schmitt, Bodo Seifert, Jerry Lopez, Mario Bijelic, Krzysztof Pennar, Felix Heide. Torc Robotics & TÜV Rheinland. SAE 2025-01-8078 · Template on GitHub.

What they did. Adapted automotive Process FMEA (PFMEA) to the ML development pipeline, treating ML as a process rather than a model. They map each of eleven pipeline steps — from data collection through model deployment and feedback analysis — to specific failure modes with associated mitigations drawn from ML best practices. The paper ships with an open-source ML FMEA Template (linked above) that development teams can adapt directly.

Why this matters. ISO 21448 (SOTIF), UL 4600, and ISO PAS 8800 all require FMEA-style analysis on ML components — none of them prescribe how to do it. This paper fills that prescriptive gap with a template a safety engineer can actually run. The TÜV Rheinland co-authorship matters too: the certification side validated the method, not just the developer side.

REF /04

COBIT mapped to the AI lifecycle

ISACA · Jan 2025

Leveraging COBIT for Effective AI System Governance. ISACA white paper. isaca.org.

What they did. Mapped the COBIT 2019 governance framework — its five domains (EDM, APO, BAI, DSS, MEA) and lifecycle structure — onto the AI lifecycle from design through monitoring. The paper walks each domain through AI-specific considerations: strategic alignment, risk management, resource optimisation, performance measurement. It's positioned as a practitioner-oriented guide for organisations that already use COBIT for IT governance and want to extend it to AI.

Why this matters. Most AI governance discussions assume the organisation is building governance from scratch. Most enterprises aren't — they already have COBIT, ITIL, or similar in place, and need AI governance that extends what's there. This paper is the institutional reference for that extension. Worth knowing it's ISACA's positioning of its own framework — useful, but read with the same care you'd read any first-party document.

05 Auditing AI Systems

Standards and artefacts for AI audit practice.

A growing reference for AI audit work — the standards that govern how audits run, and the publicly-available artefacts auditors use. Maintained quarterly. ISO 42001 Lead Auditor certification in progress; freelance audit availability from late 2026.

Standards Governing AI Audit

The standards an AI auditor works with — both the procedural ones (how audits operate) and the AI-specific ones (what gets audited).

  • PROCEDURE
    ISO/IEC

    ISO/IEC 17021-1 — Conformity Assessment Bodies

    Requirements for bodies providing audit and certification of management systems. The standard that governs how certification bodies themselves operate — competence, impartiality, audit time, documentation.

    Foundational. Every accredited AIMS certification audit is conducted under 17021-1's umbrella. Anyone working with certified third-party assessments needs to understand the rules certification bodies are themselves bound by.

  • PROCEDURE
    ISO

    ISO 19011:2026 — Guidelines for Auditing Management Systems

    The procedural backbone for management system audits — planning, conducting, reporting, follow-up. Auditor competence requirements. Applies across ISO management system standards (9001, 14001, 27001, 42001). The 2026 edition (published May 27, 2026, replacing ISO 19011:2018) significantly modernises guidance for remote and hybrid audits, strengthens risk-based audit design, and broadens applicability. No transition period — applies immediately on publication.

    Procedural literacy starts here. The audit-engagement methodology that all management system audits run on. If you understand 19011, the differences between auditing a quality system and auditing an AI system reduce to the AI-specific standard, not the procedure. The 2026 revision is a technical update, not a structural overhaul — existing audit programmes based on 2018 do not need wholesale redesign.

  • AI-SPECIFIC
    ISO/IEC

    ISO/IEC 42006:2025 — AIMS Audit & Certification Bodies

    The audit-side companion to ISO/IEC 42001. Specifies additional requirements (on top of 17021-1) for bodies certifying AI management systems — auditor competence, audit time calculation, AI-specific assessment activities. Published 2025.

    The first dedicated benchmark for AIMS certification bodies. The transition to 42006-accredited AIMS certification is happening now across accreditation bodies (SCC Canada, UKAS, ANAB). Without it, an AIMS audit is just an ISO 19011 audit with AI words.

  • AI QUALITY
    ISO/IEC TS

    ISO/IEC TS 25058:2024 — AI System Quality Evaluation

    Technical Specification under the SQuaRE family. Guidance for evaluating AI system quality — applies the SQuaRE framework (used widely for software quality) to AI-specific characteristics. A full standard (ISO/IEC 25058) is in development and will replace this.

    The "what to measure" side of AI audit. ISO 42001 says you must monitor performance; TS 25058 gives you a quality model to anchor what monitoring actually looks like. Useful pairing for evidence-gathering during AI audits.

  • SAFETY
    ISO/IEC TR

    ISO/IEC TR 5469:2024 — Functional Safety and AI Systems

    Technical Report on AI in safety-related functions. Three uses: AI inside a safety function, non-AI safety functions protecting AI-controlled equipment, AI used to design safety functions. Builds on IEC 61508 functional safety principles.

    Audit-relevant for safety-critical AI in regulated industries — automotive, medical devices, industrial automation. Pairs with ISO 21448 (SOTIF) and ISO PAS 8800 for any AI safety case. The reference that connects classical functional safety practice to learning-enabled systems.

Audit Artefacts & References

Publicly-available templates, playbooks, and frameworks AI auditors draw on. Vendor-neutral; mostly from public bodies and major research institutions.

  • PLAYBOOK
    NIST

    NIST AI RMF Playbook

    Companion to the NIST AI Risk Management Framework. Concrete suggested actions for each Function (Govern, Map, Measure, Manage), with sub-categories an auditor can walk through as a checklist of expected practices.

    The closest thing to a public-domain audit checklist for NIST AI RMF maturity assessment. Useful in cross-jurisdictional audit work where NIST RMF maturity is being assessed alongside ISO 42001 conformance.

  • IMPACT
    MICROSOFT

    Microsoft Responsible AI Impact Assessment Template

    A structured template for documenting AI system impact — intended use, stakeholders, harms, mitigations, fitness for purpose. Released alongside Microsoft's Responsible AI Standard v2.

    One of the few publicly-released impact assessment templates from a major AI vendor. Useful both as an audit artefact (evidence of impact assessment) and as a comparator when reviewing an organisation's own template.

  • THREAT REPORT
    ENISA

    ENISA — AI Cybersecurity Challenges & Threat Landscape

    European Union Agency for Cybersecurity's threat landscape for AI systems. Catalogues threats across the AI lifecycle — data poisoning, model evasion, model theft, supply chain — with mappings to defence taxonomies.

    The European complement to MITRE ATLAS, with regulatory weight inside the EU AI Act ecosystem. Audit-relevant where the assessment includes AI-specific cybersecurity controls under Article 15 of the EU AI Act.

  • CROSSWALK
    NIST

    NIST AI RMF ↔ ISO/IEC 42001 Crosswalk

    Formal mapping between NIST AI RMF functions/categories and ISO/IEC 42001 clauses. Published by NIST AIRC, ~30 pages, machine-readable mapping table included.

    The compliance-grade reference for harmonising two governance regimes. Where an audit covers organisations subject to both NIST RMF expectations and seeking ISO 42001 certification, this is the document that establishes which controls satisfy both.

07 Field Notes

What I read, track, and refer back to — beyond the headline frameworks.

A working library of the things that don't fit neatly into a CV. Frameworks I draw from when ISO 42001 alone doesn't reach far enough. Regulatory moves outside the EU AI Act that will shape the next five years. Curated, not exhaustive.

Beyond the headline frameworks.

ISO 42001 and NIST AI RMF cover the management system. These are the references I reach for when the headline frameworks aren't enough — split here into the prescriptive (what to do, test for, defend against) and the empirical (what's been measured and documented in practice).

Frameworks & Standards

Prescriptive references — what to do, what to test for, what to defend against.

  • STANDARD
    ISO/IEC

    ISO/IEC 23894 — AI Risk Management Guidance

    A companion to ISO 31000, specialised for AI. Not a certification standard — guidance for integrating AI-specific risk into existing enterprise risk processes.

    It's the bridge between ISO 31000 (general risk) and ISO 42001 (AI management system). Most teams skip it; treating it as the connective tissue is what makes the unified control system actually unified.

  • FRAMEWORK
    ISACA

    COBIT — Control Objectives for IT

    An IT governance and management framework from ISACA. Older than the AI conversation, but its EDM (Evaluate, Direct, Monitor) loop is exactly the shape AI governance needs.

    When organisations ask "how does AI governance fit into our existing IT governance," the honest answer is "through COBIT." It's the framework non-AI executives already understand.

  • CHECKLIST
    OWASP

    OWASP Top 10 for LLM Applications

    A community-maintained list of the most critical security risks in LLM-integrated applications. Prompt injection, data leakage, model denial of service, supply chain vulnerabilities.

    The first practical security checklist for production LLM systems. Where ISO 42001 says "you must manage AI risks," OWASP says "specifically, here's what to test for."

  • CHECKLIST
    OWASP

    OWASP Top 10 for Agentic Applications

    Released December 2025 by the OWASP GenAI Security Project. Covers agent-specific risks — goal hijack, tool misuse, identity and privilege abuse, supply chain via plugins and MCP servers, memory poisoning, multi-agent communication tampering.

    Where the LLM Top 10 covers prompt-level risk, the Agentic Top 10 covers what happens when those models can act. Microsoft's agentic failure modes reference it; NVIDIA's safety framework cites it. The vocabulary for talking about agentic risk is being set right now.

  • CHECKLIST
    OWASP

    OWASP Machine Learning Security Top 10

    A separate project covering classical ML security risks — input manipulation, data poisoning, model inversion, membership inference, model theft, model poisoning. Distinct from the LLM list, focused on the broader ML pipeline.

    Pre-dates the generative AI conversation and covers the security risks that don't go away just because the model is now a transformer. Adversarial robustness, training-data integrity, and model extraction are still problems — just under-discussed compared to prompt injection.

  • THREAT MODEL
    MITRE

    MITRE ATLAS — Adversarial Threat Landscape for AI Systems

    A knowledge base of adversarial tactics, techniques, and case studies for AI systems — structured like MITRE ATT&CK but for ML attacks.

    The most rigorous public catalogue of real-world AI attacks. When a regulator asks "what threats does this system face," ATLAS is the source you cite — not your own internal threat model.

Empirical Resources

Observational references — what the field has measured, mapped, and documented happening in practice.

  • TAXONOMY
    MIT

    MIT AI Risk Repository

    A living database of 1700+ AI risks aggregated from 74 frameworks, organised by a causal taxonomy (how, when, why) and a domain taxonomy (7 domains, 24 subdomains). Updated regularly by the MIT AI Risk Initiative.

    The closest thing to a periodic table for AI risk. When two frameworks describe what looks like the same risk in different language, this is where you check whether they actually mean the same thing.

  • INCIDENTS
    AIID

    AI Incident Database

    A public, searchable record of real-world AI harms and failures, maintained by the Responsible AI Collaborative since 2020. Each incident is sourced from news reports, organised by entity, category, and taxonomy, and citable in research and policy work.

    Everything else in this section describes what could or should happen. This one documents what actually has happened. When a regulator asks for evidence that a class of risk is real and recurring, the AIID is the public record that lets you point at incident IDs rather than gesture at headlines.

  • PROVENANCE
    MIT

    Data Provenance Initiative

    A multi-institution audit of 1,800+ AI training datasets, tracing source, licensing, attribution, and downstream use. Published in Nature Machine Intelligence (Longpre et al., 2024). The accompanying Data Provenance Explorer lets users generate structured provenance cards for individual datasets.

    The empirical evidence that training-data lineage is broken at scale — license omission rates above 70%, error rates above 50% on major dataset hosting sites. Anyone arguing about EU AI Act technical-documentation obligations should read this first; it's the dataset of the problem.

  • VALIDATION
    DKFZ

    Metrics Reloaded

    A framework and interactive tool for problem-aware metric selection in biomedical image analysis. Published as two companion papers in Nature Methods (Maier-Hein, Reinke et al., 2024). Built around the concept of a "problem fingerprint" that maps a given task to its appropriate validation metrics.

    A worked answer to the question every clinical AI deployment quietly avoids — "did we measure performance the right way." Metric selection is itself a governance decision; this is the framework that operationalises it.

Global AI regulation.

The EU AI Act dominates the conversation. It shouldn't. A jurisdictional snapshot of where else high-stakes AI rules are being written, debated, or enforced.

  • EUROPEAN UNIONAs of June '26

    EU AI Act

    The world's first comprehensive AI law. Risk-based classification (unacceptable, high, limited, minimal), with the strictest obligations on high-risk systems in healthcare, employment, critical infrastructure, and law enforcement. Prohibited uses in force since February 2025; GPAI model obligations since August 2025. On 7 May 2026, the EU reached a provisional political agreement on the Digital Omnibus on AI — the most significant change is a deferral of Annex III high-risk system obligations from 2 August 2026 to 2 December 2027. Formal adoption expected before August 2026; the original Act applies until then.

    The reference point every other jurisdiction is measured against, including by people who disagree with it. Understanding what it actually requires — versus what people think it requires — is the precondition for any serious AI governance work in Europe. The Omnibus deferral is the biggest practical news of 2026: organisations that had been racing for August compliance now have 16 more months for Annex III systems, but prohibited uses and GPAI obligations are unaffected.

  • SINGAPOREAs of June '26

    Model AI Governance Framework for Agentic AI (v1.5)

    Singapore IMDA's framework for agentic AI deployment. Version 1.0 launched at the World Economic Forum in January 2026; Version 1.5 (51 pages) published 20 May 2026, incorporating feedback from over 60 organisations and 10+ real-world case studies from AWS, DBS, Google, GovTech and others. Voluntary guidance organised around four dimensions: assess and bound risks upfront, make humans meaningfully accountable, implement technical controls, enable end-user responsibility. The May update adds substantive guidance on multi-agent systems, third-party agent risk, and automation bias.

    The world's first national framework addressing agentic systems specifically — not LLMs, not generative AI in general, but agents that plan and act. Version 1.5 moves from principles to operational playbook: it names specific structural, rule-based, and prompt-layer controls and gives practitioners concrete implementation patterns. Read alongside the OWASP Agentic Top 10, it's the governance-side companion to the security-side checklist.

  • SOUTH KOREAAs of June '26

    AI Basic Act

    Korea's foundational AI law, effective January 2026. Risk-based framework with provisions for "high-impact AI" and generative AI. Notably extraterritorial — applies to foreign providers serving the Korean market. The linked Explorer (Future of Life Institute + South Korea AISI) is the best English navigation of the Act.

    The world's second comprehensive AI law after the EU AI Act. Read the two side by side and the underlying regulatory instinct becomes legible — same problem, different legal traditions producing similar architectures.

  • UAEAs of June '26

    UAE AI Charter + CBUAE AI Principles

    Two complementary instruments. The UAE AI Charter (July 2024) is a cross-sectoral, non-binding charter of 12 principles anchored to the National AI Strategy 2031 — human oversight, transparency, algorithmic fairness, data privacy, safety, and three UAE-unique principles on technological excellence, human commitment, and peaceful coexistence. The CBUAE AI Principles (2023) are sector-specific voluntary guidance for banks, insurers, and financial institutions: the same core principles operationalised for credit scoring, fraud detection, AML/CFT, and customer onboarding, with accountability sitting at the licensed institution rather than split between provider and deployer.

    The UAE is the most instructive case of a jurisdiction moving fast without a hard law — principles-based governance deployed at national scale across government services, with sector regulators supplying the enforcement teeth. For anyone working on dual EU–UAE compliance, seven of the Charter's 12 principles map directly to EU AI Act provisions; the divergence is in conformity procedures, not underlying values. See ART /03 for the full side-by-side.

  • CHINAAs of June '26

    Interim Measures for the Management of Generative AI Services

    The CAC's binding regulation on public-facing generative AI services in mainland China, in force since August 2023. Mandatory algorithm filing, security assessments for services with "public opinion attributes," labelling requirements for synthetic content. (Linked translation by China Law Translate.)

    The only major jurisdiction enforcing AI rules with material consequences today — over 600 generative AI services have completed the CAC filing process, with hundreds more registered. The operational record, whatever one thinks of the policy choices, is data the rest of the world is studying.

  • UNITED STATESAs of June '26

    NIST AI RMF + state-level patchwork

    No comprehensive federal AI law; NIST AI RMF as voluntary federal guidance; rapidly proliferating state laws (Colorado AI Act, NYC bias audits, California disclosure rules).

    The compliance picture in the US looks nothing like the EU's. Multinationals operating in both face two governance philosophies running in parallel — the central design problem of the decade.
